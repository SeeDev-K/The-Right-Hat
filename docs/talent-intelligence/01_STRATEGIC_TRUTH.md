# The Right Hat — Phase 1: Strategic Truth

Date: 2026-07-10
Decision: **MODIFY AND PROCEED**

## Executive verdict

The broad vision is attractive but not launchable as written.

A platform that simultaneously attempts to replace LinkedIn, Indeed, Upwork, Fiverr, GitHub, Wellfound, traditional recruitment agencies, ATS software, learning platforms and professional communities will almost certainly fail through lack of focus before it benefits from platform scale.

The correct decision is not to stop the project. It is to narrow the market entry.

### Recommended launch thesis

> **The Right Hat helps companies build trusted technology teams faster by matching them with verified cybersecurity, AI, cloud and digital engineering talent based on demonstrated evidence rather than self-declared CV claims.**

### Long-term platform thesis

If the initial trust and evidence layer works, TRH can later expand into:
- permanent hiring;
- consulting and freelance missions;
- managed technical teams;
- learning and certification;
- internal mobility;
- co-founder and expert matching;
- talent intelligence APIs;
- international mobility;
- broader professional communities.

The sequence matters. The trust layer comes before the global ecosystem.

---

## 1. The problem worth solving

The core problem is not that companies lack access to profiles. They already have LinkedIn, job boards, agencies, referrals, universities and freelance platforms.

The problem is that they lack **reliable, structured and comparable proof** that a candidate can perform the work required.

For technical and security roles, employers frequently face:
- inflated or generic CV claims;
- certifications without demonstrated ability;
- portfolios that are difficult to compare;
- inconsistent screening quality;
- long recruiter and engineering review cycles;
- weak signals for junior and non-traditional candidates;
- difficulty assessing remote collaboration readiness;
- fragmented evidence across GitHub, certificates, CVs, references and assessments;
- a high cost when a bad hire reaches production systems or sensitive data.

Candidates face the inverse problem:
- strong skills are hidden behind weak CV presentation;
- degrees and employer brands can outweigh practical ability;
- repeated assessments create fatigue;
- verified work is not portable between employers;
- regional talent may be discounted because of location or unfamiliar institutions;
- freelancers, self-taught professionals and career changers lack trusted signals.

### Strategic conclusion

TRH should not compete on access to more profiles. It should compete on **better evidence, faster trust and more explainable matching**.

---

## 2. Why now

The timing is favorable for a focused technology-talent platform.

Signals supporting the opportunity include:
- rapid employer adoption of AI-assisted sourcing and screening;
- increasing demand for AI, data, cloud and cybersecurity skills;
- movement toward skills-based hiring rather than degree-only filters;
- expansion of remote and cross-border technology work;
- Morocco's public investment in digital talent and AI capacity;
- growing employer concern about candidate authenticity and AI-generated applications;
- candidate frustration with opaque automated hiring systems.

Current market evidence also shows that incumbents are moving aggressively into AI recruitment. LinkedIn's AI hiring products were reported in 2026 as being on track for substantial annual revenue, which proves willingness to pay but also means that generic AI sourcing is not an open market by itself.

Morocco is simultaneously investing in digital and AI capability. Public targets reported in 2025-2026 include substantial expansion of the technology workforce, AI training and AI-related job creation. That creates a favorable supply-side context, but it does not automatically create a company. TRH must still prove that employers pay for its specific trust and verification workflow.

### Timing verdict

- Market timing: **strong**
- Competitive intensity: **very high**
- Opportunity for a generic job board: **weak**
- Opportunity for a regional verified technical-talent network: **credible**

## 3. Initial market selection

### Recommended geography

**Primary launch:** Morocco

**Supply expansion:** francophone Africa and Moroccan diaspora

**Demand expansion:** France, Belgium, Gulf markets and European nearshore employers

### Why Morocco first

Advantages:
- founder proximity and local understanding;
- existing TRH brand and technology community direction;
- multilingual talent pool;
- geographic and time-zone proximity to Europe;
- active national digital-skills strategy;
- ability to operate high-touch validation before full automation;
- existing local pain around youth employment and skills visibility.

Risks:
- employer budgets may be lower than European budgets;
- informal recruitment through referrals and WhatsApp is deeply embedded;
- local incumbents already have employer relationships;
- enterprise procurement can be slow;
- top talent may prefer direct access to international employers.

### Recommended launch segment

TRH should begin with four related families:
1. cybersecurity;
2. cloud and DevOps;
3. AI and data;
4. software engineering for security-sensitive or high-growth environments.

This is still broad enough to support demand, but narrow enough to build meaningful verification taxonomies and communities.

### Explicitly excluded from launch

- hospitality;
- retail and hourly work;
- general administration;
- all creative freelancing categories;
- healthcare recruitment;
- mass public-sector recruitment;
- executive search across every industry;
- a general social network for all professions.

## 4. Primary buyer and users

### Primary economic buyer

**CTO, Head of Engineering, Founder or Head of Talent at a company with recurring technical hiring and insufficient internal specialist screening capacity.**

Recommended company profile:
- 10 to 500 employees;
- startup, scale-up, technology services company, consultancy, bank, insurer, industrial group or digital transformation team;
- at least three technical hires or external expert missions per year;
- roles that take too long to validate;
- meaningful cost of a bad hire;
- openness to Moroccan, African, remote or nearshore talent.

### Secondary buyer

- specialist recruitment agencies needing better technical validation;
- universities and bootcamps seeking employability evidence;
- enterprises building internal talent marketplaces;
- public and private digital-skills initiatives.

### Talent-side user

The initial talent user is not every job seeker. It is a professional who benefits from proof-based differentiation:
- junior with strong practical projects;
- self-taught engineer;
- certified cybersecurity professional;
- cloud or DevOps specialist;
- AI or data practitioner;
- experienced professional seeking remote or international work;
- freelancer seeking higher-trust engagements;
- diaspora professional open to regional projects.

### Buyer pain hypothesis

> “We receive many applications, but our engineering and security teams still spend too much time determining who is genuinely qualified.”

### Talent pain hypothesis

> “My capability is distributed across projects, certifications, repositories and experience, but employers still reduce me to a CV and a keyword filter.”

## 5. The wedge

### Recommended wedge

**Verified Talent Passport + Explainable AI Shortlist**

The Talent Passport is a reusable professional trust profile composed of evidence units rather than one opaque score.

Evidence units may include:
- verified identity;
- employment and mission history;
- certification verification;
- education verification;
- portfolio and repository evidence;
- project walkthroughs;
- structured technical assessments;
- peer or manager references;
- communication and availability preferences;
- language capability;
- location and mobility constraints;
- security-sensitive eligibility where lawful and consented.

The employer receives:
- a structured role brief;
- an explainable shortlist;
- evidence supporting each recommendation;
- identified evidence gaps;
- interview focus areas;
- candidate consent and availability status.

### What the wedge is not

It is not:
- a universal reputation score;
- an AI personality score;
- a black-box culture-fit ranking;
- an automated hiring decision;
- another CV database;
- another public feed;
- a generic chatbot attached to a job board.

### Why this wedge can be defensible

The interface and matching algorithm are copyable. The defensible asset is the accumulated combination of:
- verified evidence;
- structured skills graph;
- role-to-evidence mappings;
- assessment quality data;
- interview outcomes;
- employer feedback;
- successful engagement history;
- regional labor-market context;
- trusted community and institutional partnerships.

This moat exists only if TRH collects high-quality outcome data with consent. A feature list alone is not a moat.

## 6. Competitive reality

TRH enters a crowded market.

### Global pressure

- LinkedIn owns professional identity and recruiter distribution.
- Indeed owns large-scale job discovery.
- Upwork owns global freelance transaction history.
- Andela specializes in vetted global technical talent.
- Wellfound owns a recognizable startup hiring position.
- Deel and Remote own cross-border employment infrastructure.
- ATS providers already sit inside employer workflows.

### Regional pressure

Morocco already has established and emerging recruitment platforms, including ReKrute, Emploi.ma, Jobzyn, ANAPEC-related workflows and specialist vertical sites. Employers also rely heavily on referrals, agencies, alumni networks and direct outreach.

### Competitive conclusion

TRH cannot win by saying:
- “we also use AI”;
- “we have jobs and profiles”;
- “we combine several platforms”;
- “our UX is more modern.”

TRH can potentially win a narrow segment by saying:

> “For hard-to-validate technology roles, we produce a trusted, evidence-backed shortlist and reduce the burden on your engineering and security teams.”

## 7. Founder-market fit

### Existing advantages

The founder profile creates a plausible right to play:
- cybersecurity and systems architecture experience;
- GovTech and digital-platform work;
- understanding of Morocco and Belgium/European contexts;
- ability to design trust, identity and security workflows;
- existing TRH brand direction around cybersecurity and community;
- access to technical networks and institutional conversations.

### Missing proof

These advantages do not yet prove:
- repeatable employer distribution;
- recruitment operations expertise;
- candidate acquisition at scale;
- assessment validity;
- willingness to pay;
- successful placement economics.

### Founder-market fit verdict

**Promising but unproven.**

The founder's strongest advantage is not generic HR knowledge. It is the ability to build a trusted technical-talent system where cybersecurity, identity and evidence are first-class product concepts.

The team should add or contract:
- a senior technical recruiter;
- an occupational assessment specialist;
- a labor and data-protection legal adviser;
- a B2B sales operator with Moroccan and European employer access.

## 8. MVP definition

### MVP objective

Prove that employers will pay for an evidence-backed shortlist and that strong talent will complete a reusable verification profile.

### MVP components

1. **Talent onboarding**
   - identity and consent;
   - structured experience;
   - skills selection;
   - availability and work preferences.

2. **Evidence vault**
   - CV;
   - certificates;
   - links and portfolios;
   - project evidence;
   - references;
   - verification status per item.

3. **Verified Talent Passport**
   - public, private and employer-only visibility controls;
   - clear verified, self-declared and pending labels;
   - no global human score.

4. **Employer role brief**
   - outcomes, tasks and environment;
   - required versus trainable skills;
   - compensation and work model;
   - explicit deal-breakers;
   - interview process.

5. **Matching workspace**
   - semantic search;
   - evidence-backed recommendations;
   - reason for fit;
   - missing evidence;
   - human approval.

6. **Recruiter pipeline**
   - shortlisted;
   - contacted;
   - interested;
   - interview;
   - offer;
   - hired or mission started;
   - outcome feedback.

7. **Concierge operations layer**
   - manual verification;
   - manual candidate interviews;
   - human-reviewed shortlists;
   - direct support for design partners.

### Features excluded from MVP

- social feed;
- payments and escrow;
- time tracking;
- video interview infrastructure;
- full ATS replacement;
- autonomous recruiter contacting candidates without approval;
- salary prediction presented as fact;
- personality-based ranking;
- marketplace advertising;
- leaderboards;
- hundreds of learning courses;
- investor marketplace;
- startup co-founder matching.

## 9. Cold-start strategy

A public marketplace launch is not recommended.

### Supply first, but curated

Build an initial pool of 100 to 300 relevant professionals through:
- TRH community;
- cybersecurity and developer communities;
- universities and bootcamps;
- certification cohorts;
- founder networks;
- diaspora communities;
- referrals from respected practitioners.

Verify deeply only the first 30 to 50 profiles. Quality matters more than volume.

### Demand through design partners

Recruit 10 design-partner employers with active or recurring technical needs.

Offer a concierge pilot:
1. employer submits a role;
2. TRH restructures it into a skills and evidence brief;
3. TRH sources and verifies candidates;
4. employer receives three to five evidence-backed profiles;
5. TRH records interview and outcome feedback.

### Why concierge first

The first version must learn:
- which evidence employers trust;
- which assessments predict interview success;
- why candidates drop out;
- how much human work is required;
- what employers will pay for;
- where matching explanations fail.

Automating before learning these workflows would create polished software around unproven assumptions.

## 10. Business model hypotheses

These are experiments, not established pricing recommendations.

### Pilot A — Verified shortlist

- fixed role activation or shortlist fee;
- optional success fee after hire;
- suitable for SMEs and startups.

### Pilot B — Technical validation service

- employer sends existing finalists;
- TRH verifies evidence and structures technical review;
- useful before TRH has enough marketplace liquidity.

### Pilot C — Recruiter workspace

- monthly access after repeated usage;
- search, saved talent, pipelines and verification status;
- only after employers demonstrate recurring demand.

### Indicative pricing experiments

Test, do not assume:
- 5,000 to 15,000 MAD for a concierge verified-shortlist pilot;
- success fee based on role complexity and annual compensation;
- 2,500 to 7,500 MAD monthly for a small-team recruiter workspace after product value is proven;
- enterprise pricing by seats, active roles and verification volume.

Candidate access should be free for core participation. Paid candidate tools may later include coaching, premium portfolio support or optional assessments, but payment must never become a hidden requirement for employment visibility.

## 11. AI strategy for the first product

### AI should perform

- CV and document structuring;
- skills normalization;
- role-brief generation;
- semantic retrieval;
- evidence-to-requirement mapping;
- interview question suggestions;
- profile summaries;
- contradiction and missing-evidence detection;
- multilingual assistance in French, English, Arabic and eventually Darija;
- recruiter workflow assistance.

### AI should not perform alone

- final rejection;
- fraud accusations;
- personality judgment;
- culture-fit exclusion;
- sensitive-trait inference;
- compensation decisions;
- irreversible candidate ranking without review.

### AI product principle

Every recommendation must answer:
1. Why was this person recommended?
2. Which evidence supports the recommendation?
3. What is uncertain or missing?
4. Which human must approve the next action?

## 12. Thirty-day validation sprint

### Week 1 — Problem interviews

Complete:
- 15 CTO/founder/engineering-leader interviews;
- 10 HR/recruiter interviews;
- 20 talent interviews.

Do not pitch features first. Document:
- last difficult hire;
- time spent;
- channels used;
- screening stages;
- failure points;
- cost of delay;
- cost of bad hire;
- evidence trusted;
- budget owner.

### Week 2 — Concierge prototype

Create manually:
- role brief template;
- Talent Passport prototype;
- evidence taxonomy for two roles;
- recruiter shortlist format;
- consent model;
- employer feedback form.

Recommended first role archetypes:
- SOC analyst / cybersecurity analyst;
- cloud or DevOps engineer.

### Week 3 — Design partners

Secure:
- five employers with live or expected roles;
- 50 interested talents;
- 20 completed structured profiles;
- 10 deeply verified profiles.

### Week 4 — Paid evidence

Attempt to close:
- at least three paid pilots, deposits or signed letters of intent containing a budget and deployment date;
- at least two completed shortlists;
- employer feedback on every recommendation;
- candidate feedback on verification burden and trust.

## 13. Validation KPIs

### Problem validation

- percentage of employers reporting repeated technical screening pain;
- hours of engineering time spent per role;
- average time to a credible shortlist;
- percentage already paying agencies or tools;
- named budget owner.

### Product validation

- profile completion rate;
- evidence verification completion rate;
- candidate drop-off by step;
- employer acceptance rate of recommended candidates;
- shortlist-to-interview rate;
- interview-to-offer rate;
- time to shortlist;
- employer trust rating;
- candidate trust rating.

### Commercial validation

- paid pilot conversion;
- average pilot revenue;
- employer repeat intent;
- cost to verify one talent;
- human operations time per role;
- gross margin per engagement.

### Initial target hypotheses

These are targets for learning, not promises:
- qualified shortlist in 72 hours to 7 days depending on role;
- shortlist-to-interview rate above 35%;
- at least 40% employer repeat intent after a completed pilot;
- at least 60% verified-profile completion among accepted talent applicants;
- evidence verification cost declining with every cohort.

---

## 14. Kill criteria

Pause or materially change the concept if, after structured testing:

1. employers describe the pain but will not pay for verification or shortlists;
2. strong candidates consistently refuse the verification process;
3. employers only want low-cost job posting volume;
4. TRH recommendations do not outperform ordinary recruiter sourcing;
5. no employer repeats after the first pilot;
6. verification costs remain too high for sustainable margins;
7. evidence collected does not improve interview outcomes;
8. employer demand is too fragmented across unrelated roles;
9. the strongest distribution channel belongs entirely to partners who capture the margin;
10. compliance and consent requirements make the intended data model unacceptable.

Stopping a weak version is not failure. It protects the company from spending years building a marketplace without liquidity.
